# Problem Statement

Enterprise security operations centers (SOCs) face a persistent operational crisis: the volume of security alerts far exceeds the capacity of human analysts to investigate them thoroughly. A typical mid-size organization generates thousands of alerts per day from endpoint detection, network monitoring, identity systems, and cloud workloads. Analysts must triage each signal, correlate logs across siloed tools, map activity to threat frameworks, assess business risk, draft response recommendations, and communicate findings to executives — often under time pressure while an active incident unfolds.

Manual incident response introduces structural delays at every stage. Log collection requires navigating multiple consoles. Threat intelligence lookups are performed indicator-by-indicator. MITRE ATT&CK mapping depends on analyst memory and experience. Risk narratives are written from scratch. Response playbooks are assembled ad hoc. Executive summaries are produced late, after technical analysis is complete. These sequential manual steps compound into hours of mean time to respond (MTTR), during which adversaries may expand access, exfiltrate data, or deploy ransomware.

Alert fatigue amplifies the problem. When analysts face hundreds of low-confidence alerts daily, true incidents are missed or deprioritized. Inconsistent investigation quality follows: junior analysts may skip enrichment steps; senior analysts become bottlenecks; institutional knowledge is not captured in repeatable workflows. Security teams spend disproportionate time on documentation and coordination rather than decisive action.

AI agents address these challenges by encoding investigation expertise into coordinated, repeatable pipelines. Instead of a single monolithic model, specialist agents can each own one investigation stage — evidence normalization, indicator enrichment, framework mapping, risk scoring, response planning, and executive reporting — while a coordinator ensures correct sequencing and a safety layer validates outputs before they reach analysts. Agents can operate with deterministic fallbacks when cloud AI is unavailable, preserving demo and offline capability. Human analysts remain in control of consequential decisions; agents accelerate structured analysis, reduce repetitive work, and produce consistent artifacts that shorten investigation cycles and improve organizational readiness.

The business case is direct: faster containment reduces breach cost, standardized analysis improves audit readiness, and executive-ready reporting closes the gap between technical findings and leadership decision-making. Oz AI targets this problem domain — enterprise incident response — where multi-agent AI delivers measurable workflow acceleration without replacing human judgment.
